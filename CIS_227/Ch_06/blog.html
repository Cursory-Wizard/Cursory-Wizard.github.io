Best of the Best Practices

Honing your requirements documentation, Sept. 26, 2018

For today’s post we’re going to discuss requirements documentation and what I consider to be the best practices for creating one that is effective, adaptable, and thorough. Project documentation is demanding work but it’s vital to the success of your software and the advancement of your own skills as a software designer. Setting out to develop a product without a strong guiding principle is the fastest way to produce a muddled design mired in complicated, difficult to maintain code. With these best practices you will have a strong start and look forward to an equally strong finish. Let’s get started, shall we?


Identify your stakeholders – No product is designed with ‘no one’ in mind and no project is started because ‘no one’ requested it. I can hear you say: we already know who the stakeholders are, they’re the people sitting in front of us asking for our help! Unfortunately, that is incorrect. Those are your customers. They are certainly included in your stakeholders, but they represent only the tiniest portion of your eventual end-users. However, they are the final decision-makers and their input matters most. Assist them in identifying the other stakeholders. Consider asking these five questions to help find them.

As you can see from the article, even a small medical practice has innumerable persons upon which they rely, people that may use this product and will have to be included in a requirements documentation to make it a complete success. Using this example, lead your customers through their stakeholders and help them identify each one.


Define the scope of the requirements process – Framework, framework, framework. Repeat it. Stakeholders will range in accessibility: Your customers may be easy to interact with, but they may have a proposed customer base that is nebulous and hard to qualify. The framework of the process needs to identify a reporting mechanism that ensures rigid documentation. It must include source, reliability, and accountability. Lastly, it needs to include a termination aspect where both you and the customer resolve that the requirements from that stakeholder have been identified. A good requirements process is the difference between ‘The stakeholders tell us’ and the much more inaccurate ‘We have heard…’. Make your documentation framework integral now and it will be effortless to modify later (and modify it you will!).


Model your requirements – Defined, detailed interpretations of the requirements are essential in setting both goals and expectations. Your own design team needs a complete understanding of what the customer is asking for and modeling is the most effective way to bring the two groups together.

Unified Modeling Language(UML) is your friend, here. Working from a trackable, easy to read, UML table will provide both your customers and design team with a clear description of your requirements. If you want to practice, head over to draw.io and select ‘UML’. You’ll be presented with several templates that fit nearly every situation. Working in a UML document eliminates abstract goals by necessitating a thorough outline of each requirement.


Prioritize your requirements – Not every idea is equal, and time is not infinite. Your customers and stakeholders will have an extensive list at this point. Hopefully, your design team has a clear idea of them, thanks to the UML. Now it is time to prioritize and set expectations.

This is often a painful process because everyone wants to be involved. You may find that each person has a handful of requirements they want to see included and aren’t very much concerned with the rest. Your customer has seen their initial requirements balloon into something they may not be capable of encompassing and it may have them concerned. Clearly, you need a method of ranking requirements that will keep everyone involved.

There are numerable ways to prioritize a requirements list. My personal suggestion to you is that this decision is one of the reasons you are a project leader. Either make it yourself or in conjunction with a small management team. Don’t stress the process by adding a decision about how to make decisions. Whether it be MoSCoW, AHP, or bubble sort (my personal favorite for smaller lists), choose one and use it to determine your final requirements list.


Finalize and capture lessons learned – That’s it, you’re done! Time to sign off on the requirements document, get it off your desktop, and move on to the process of building the product. Right? Right?

I mean…you could. But in doing so you will be missing the most important lesson of documentation and hobbling yourself when that same requirements document comes around again (you know it will).

How complete is this document? What were the accomplishments? Where do you feel the process could have been improved? Why were these missteps made?

Ask these questions and document the answers. The requirements document is a living document and will change with each iteration of the product. Keeping a record of your process and your findings is immeasurably important to your continued success and the support of your customers.


A requirements document is a difficult beast to master but it’s often the single thing standing between your team and success with a product. There are many, many other lessons and other best practices; these five only represent mine. Keeping expanding your methods and continue to refine your process. Every small win you have is a contributor to great achievement in the end.
